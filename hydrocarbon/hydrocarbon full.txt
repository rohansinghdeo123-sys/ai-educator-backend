PART 1: HYDROCARBONS — INTRODUCTION & ALKANES
SECTION 1: WHAT ARE HYDROCARBONS
Notes

Hydrocarbons are organic compounds made only of two elements:

Carbon (C)

Hydrogen (H)

They are called hydrocarbons because:

“Hydro” means hydrogen

“Carbon” means carbon

Hydrocarbons are the basic compounds from which many other organic compounds are formed.

Classification of hydrocarbons:

Saturated hydrocarbons → Alkanes

Unsaturated hydrocarbons → Alkenes and Alkynes

Aromatic hydrocarbons → Benzene and its derivatives

SECTION 2: ALKANES (SATURATED HYDROCARBONS)
Notes

Alkanes are saturated hydrocarbons. This means:

They contain only single bonds between carbon atoms

They do not contain double or triple bonds

General formula of alkanes:
CnH2n+2

Examples:

Methane: CH4

Ethane: C2H6

Propane: C3H8

First few members of alkane series:

Methane (CH4)

Ethane (C2H6)

Propane (C3H8)

Butane (C4H10)

Alkanes are also called paraffins, which means “little reactive”.

Boiling point increases

Density increases

SECTION 4: CHEMICAL PROPERTIES OF ALKANES
(A) Combustion
Notes

Alkanes burn in oxygen to form:

Carbon dioxide (CO2)

Water (H2O)

Heat and light

General reaction:
Hydrocarbon + Oxygen -> CO2 + H2O

Example:
CH4 + 2O2 -> CO2 + 2H2O

This reaction releases energy, therefore alkanes are used as fuels.

(B) Substitution Reaction
Notes

In alkanes, hydrogen atoms can be replaced by other atoms such as chlorine or bromine.
This type of reaction is called a substitution reaction.

Example:
CH4 + Cl2 -> CH3Cl + HCl (in sunlight)

Only one hydrogen atom is replaced at a time.

SECTION 6: CRACKING OF ALKANES
Notes

Cracking is the process of breaking large hydrocarbon molecules into smaller and more useful hydrocarbons.

Cracking is carried out by:

Heating at high temperature

Using catalysts such as Al2O3 or SiO2

Types of cracking:

Thermal cracking

Catalytic cracking

Uses of cracking:

Increases petrol yield

Produces alkenes for polymer manufacture

Example:
C10H22 -> C5H12 + C5H10

PART 2: ALKENES
SECTION 1: WHAT ARE ALKENES

Notes

Alkenes are unsaturated hydrocarbons that contain at least one carbon–carbon double bond (C=C).

Because of the presence of a double bond, alkenes are more reactive than alkanes.

General formula of alkenes:
CnH2n

Examples:

Ethene: C2H4

Propene: C3H6

Alkenes are also called olefins, which means “oil-forming compounds”.

Easy MCQs

Which of the following is an alkene?
A. CH4
B. C2H6
C. C2H4
D. C3H8
Correct Answer: C

SECTION 2: STRUCTURE OF DOUBLE BOND

Notes

A carbon–carbon double bond consists of:

One sigma (sigma) bond – strong

One pi (pi) bond – weak

Sigma bond is formed by head-on overlap of orbitals.
Pi bond is formed by sideways overlap of p-orbitals.

Because the pi bond is weak, it breaks easily, allowing alkenes to undergo addition reactions.

Bond length:

C=C bond is shorter than a C–C single bond

SECTION 3: NOMENCLATURE OF ALKENES (IUPAC)

Notes

Rules for naming alkenes:

Choose the longest carbon chain containing the double bond

Number the chain from the end nearest to the double bond

Use the suffix “ene”

Examples:

CH2=CH2 → Ethene

CH3–CH=CH2 → Prop-1-ene

CH3–CH=CH–CH3 → But-2-ene

SECTION 8: MARKOVNIKOV’S RULE

Notes

Markovnikov’s rule applies to unsymmetrical alkenes.

Rule:

Hydrogen attaches to the carbon atom with more hydrogen atoms

Example:
Propene + HBr → 2-Bromopropane (major product)

Does not work with HCl or HI

Propene + HBr (peroxide) → 1-Bromopropane

Notes

Alkynes are unsaturated hydrocarbons that contain at least one carbon–carbon triple bond (C≡C).

Because of the triple bond:

Alkynes are more reactive than alkenes and alkanes

General formula of alkynes:
CnH₂n₋₂

Examples:

Ethyne (Acetylene): C₂H₂

Propyne: C₃H₄

The first member of the alkyne series is ethyne.

--- SECTION 2: STRUCTURE OF TRIPLE BOND ---
Notes

A carbon–carbon triple bond consists of:

One sigma (σ) bond

Two pi (π) bonds

Sigma bond is strong.
Pi bonds are weak and break easily.

In alkynes:

Carbon atoms are sp hybridised

Shape is linear

Bond angle is 180°

PART 4: AROMATIC HYDROCARBONS (BENZENE & ARENES)
SECTION 1: WHAT ARE AROMATIC HYDROCARBONS
Notes

Aromatic hydrocarbons are a special class of hydrocarbons that contain a benzene ring or a similar ring structure.

They are called arenes.

These compounds show extra stability, which is called aromaticity.

Examples of aromatic hydrocarbons:

Benzene (C6H6)

Toluene

Naphthalene

Aromatic hydrocarbons are more stable than alkenes because their electrons are delocalised over the ring.

SECTION 2: STRUCTURE OF BENZENE
Notes

The molecular formula of benzene is C6H6.

Benzene has:

Six carbon atoms

Six hydrogen atoms

Each carbon atom is sp2 hybridised.

The molecule is planar and forms a hexagonal ring.

Kekulé suggested alternating double bonds, but the actual structure is a resonance hybrid.

All carbon–carbon bonds in benzene are equal in length.

SECTION 3: RESONANCE AND STABILITY OF BENZENE
Notes

Benzene shows resonance.

The pi electrons are delocalised over the entire ring.

This delocalisation provides extra stability, called resonance energy.

Because of this stability, benzene does not prefer addition reactions.

Instead, benzene mainly undergoes substitution reactions.

SECTION 4: AROMATICITY (HUCKEL’S RULE)
Notes

A compound is aromatic if it satisfies all of the following conditions:

It is cyclic

It is planar

It is fully conjugated

It contains (4n + 2) pi electrons

This rule is called Huckel’s rule.

Examples:

Benzene → 6 pi electrons

Naphthalene → 10 pi electrons
