# AROMATIC HYDROCARBONS (ARENES)

---

## 1️⃣ Definition

- Aromatic hydrocarbons are also called **arenes**.
- Most contain one or more **benzene rings**.
- Benzene ring is highly unsaturated but behaves differently from alkenes.

---

## Benzenoids and Non-Benzenoids

- **Benzenoids** → Contain benzene ring  
- **Non-benzenoids** → Aromatic but do not contain benzene ring  

---

## Examples of Arenes

- Benzene (C6H6)
- Toluene (Methylbenzene)
- Naphthalene
- Biphenyl
- Anthracene
- Phenanthrene

---

## 2️⃣ Nomenclature and Isomerism

### Monosubstituted Benzene

All 6 hydrogens in benzene are equivalent → Only **one monosubstituted product** is possible.

Example:  
CH3–benzene → Methylbenzene (Toluene)

---

### Disubstituted Benzene

When two substituents are present → 3 position isomers possible:

| Position | Name | Symbol |
|----------|------|--------|
| 1,2 | Ortho | o- |
| 1,3 | Meta | m- |
| 1,4 | Para | p- |

Example (Dimethylbenzene):
- 1,2-dimethylbenzene → o-xylene  
- 1,3-dimethylbenzene → m-xylene  
- 1,4-dimethylbenzene → p-xylene  

---

## 3️⃣ Structure of Benzene

### Molecular Formula: C6H6

- Indicates high unsaturation
- Shows unusual stability

### Kekulé Structure

- Alternating single and double bonds
- Two possible structures

### Resonance Structure

- Benzene is a **resonance hybrid**
- π electrons are delocalised
- Represented by a circle inside hexagon

### Important Structural Features

- All C–C bond lengths equal (139 pm)
- Planar molecule
- Each carbon is sp2 hybridised
- Bond angle = 120°

---

## 4️⃣ Aromaticity

A compound is aromatic if:

1. It is cyclic
2. It is planar
3. It has complete delocalisation of π electrons
4. It follows Hückel’s Rule: (4n + 2) π electrons

Examples:
- Benzene → 6 π electrons (n=1)
- Naphthalene → 10 π electrons (n=2)

---

# PREPARATION OF BENZENE

## 1. From Coal Tar

Obtained commercially from coal tar.

## 2. Cyclic Polymerisation of Ethyne

3HC≡CH → Benzene  
(873 K, red hot iron tube)

## 3. Decarboxylation

C6H5COONa + NaOH → C6H6 + Na2CO3  
(CaO, heat)

## 4. Reduction of Phenol

Phenol + Zn → Benzene + ZnO

# PHYSICAL PROPERTIES

- Non-polar
- Colourless liquids or solids
- Characteristic smell
- Insoluble in water
- Soluble in organic solvents
- Burn with sooty flame

2C6H6 + 15O2 → 12CO2 + 6H2O

---

# CHEMICAL PROPERTIES

Benzene prefers substitution reactions.

## Electrophilic Substitution

### Nitration
Benzene + HNO3 + H2SO4 → Nitrobenzene + H2O

### Halogenation
Benzene + Cl2 → Chlorobenzene + HCl (AlCl3)

### Sulphonation
Benzene + H2SO4 → Benzene sulphonic acid

### Friedel–Crafts Alkylation
Benzene + CH3Cl → Toluene (AlCl3)

### Friedel–Crafts Acylation
Benzene + CH3COCl → Acetophenone (AlCl3)

## Addition (Special Conditions)

Benzene + 3H2 → Cyclohexane (Ni)

Benzene + 3Cl2 → Benzene hexachloride (UV)

---

## Directive Influence

### Ortho/Para Directing (Activating)
–OH, –NH2, –CH3

### Meta Directing (Deactivating)
–NO2, –COOH, –CHO, –SO3H

---

## Carcinogenicity

Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons can cause cancer.
