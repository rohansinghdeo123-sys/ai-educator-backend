# ALKENES -- Short & Structured Notes

## 1. Definition of Alkenes

-   Alkenes are **unsaturated hydrocarbons**.
-   They contain at least one **double bond (C=C)**.
-   Also called **olefins (oil-forming)**.

### General Formula

If one double bond is present:

CₙH₂ₙ

Reason: Compared to alkanes (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂), alkenes have 2 hydrogen atoms
less.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. Structure of Double Bond

A carbon--carbon double bond consists of:

-   One sigma (σ) bond
-   One pi (π) bond

### Sigma Bond

-   Formed by head-on overlap
-   Strong bond
-   Bond enthalpy ≈ 397 kJ mol⁻¹

### Pi Bond

-   Formed by sideways overlap of 2p orbitals
-   Weaker bond
-   Bond enthalpy ≈ 284 kJ mol⁻¹

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Important Bond Details

-   C=C bond length = 134 pm
-   C--C single bond length = 154 pm
-   Total bond enthalpy of C=C ≈ 681 kJ mol⁻¹
-   C--C single bond ≈ 348 kJ mol⁻¹

------------------------------------------------------------------------

### Important Concept

-   π bond is weaker → electrons are loosely held
-   Alkenes are attacked by electrophiles
-   Alkenes are more reactive than alkanes

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 3. Nomenclature of Alkenes (IUPAC Rules)

1.  Select longest carbon chain containing double bond.
2.  Number from end nearer to double bond.
3.  Replace suffix "-ane" with "-ene".

### Examples

  Structure              IUPAC Name
  ---------------------- --------------------
  CH₃--CH=CH₂            Propene
  CH₃--CH₂--CH=CH₂       But-1-ene
  CH₃--CH=CH--CH₃        But-2-ene
  CH₂=CH--CH=CH₂         Buta-1,3-diene
  CH₂=C(CH₃)CH₃          2-Methylprop-1-ene
  CH₂=CH--CH(CH₃)--CH₃   3-Methylbut-1-ene

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 4. Isomerism in Alkenes

Alkenes show: - Structural isomerism - Geometrical (cis-trans) isomerism

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 5. Geometrical (Cis-Trans) Isomerism

### Condition

Each carbon of C=C must have two different groups attached.

### Cis Isomer

-   Identical groups on same side
-   More polar
-   Higher dipole moment

Example: cis-But-2-ene (μ = 0.33 D)

### Trans Isomer

-   Identical groups on opposite sides
-   Less polar
-   Dipole moment ≈ 0

Example: trans-But-2-ene

### Comparison

-   Cis → More polar
-   Trans → Higher melting point (better packing)

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# PREPARATION OF ALKENES

Alkenes are mainly prepared by elimination reactions.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 1. From Alkyl Halides (Dehydrohalogenation)

**Reagent:** Alcoholic KOH + Heat

### Example 1

CH₃--CH₂Br → CH₂=CH₂ + HBr\
Bromoethane → Ethene

### Example 2

CH₃--CHBr--CH₃ → CH₃--CH=CH₂\
2-Bromopropane → Propene

Called: - Dehydrohalogenation - β-elimination

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. From Vicinal Dihalides

**Reagent:** Zinc (Zn)

### Example

CH₂Br--CH₂Br + Zn → CH₂=CH₂ + ZnBr₂\
1,2-Dibromoethane → Ethene

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 3. From Alcohols (Acidic Dehydration)

**Reagent:** Conc. H₂SO₄ + Heat

### Example 1

CH₃--CH₂OH → CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O\
Ethanol → Ethene

### Example 2

CH₃--CH(OH)--CH₃ → CH₃--CH=CH₂\
Propan-2-ol → Propene

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 4. From Alkynes (Partial Reduction)

### (A) Lindlar Catalyst

H₂ / Pd (poisoned)

CH₃--C≡C--CH₃ → cis-CH₃--CH=CH--CH₃\
But-2-yne → cis-But-2-ene

### (B) Na / Liquid NH₃

CH₃--C≡C--CH₃ → trans-CH₃--CH=CH--CH₃\
But-2-yne → trans-But-2-ene

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# PHYSICAL PROPERTIES OF ALKENES

## 1. Physical State

-   First 3 → Gases
-   Next → Liquids
-   Higher → Solids

## 2. Colour & Odour

-   Colourless
-   Slight sweet smell (ethene)

## 3. Solubility

-   Insoluble in water
-   Soluble in organic solvents (benzene, ether, petrol)

## 4. Boiling Point

Boiling point increases with molecular mass.\
Ethene \< Propene \< Butene

## 5. Effect of Branching

-   Straight chain → Higher boiling point
-   Branched → Lower boiling point

## 6. Polarity (Cis vs Trans)

-   Cis → More polar
-   Trans → Less polar

------------------------------------------------------------------------

# CHEMICAL PROPERTIES OF ALKENES

Alkenes mainly undergo addition reactions.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 1. Hydrogenation

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂ → CH₃--CH₃\
Ethene → Ethane

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 2. Halogen Addition

CH₂=CH₂ + Br₂ → CH₂Br--CH₂Br\
Ethene → 1,2-Dibromoethane

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 3. Addition of HX

### Markovnikov Rule

CH₃--CH=CH₂ + HBr → CH₃--CHBr--CH₃\
Propene → 2-Bromopropane

### Peroxide Effect

CH₃--CH=CH₂ + HBr (Peroxide) → CH₃--CH₂--CH₂Br\
Propene → 1-Bromopropane

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 4. Hydration

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → CH₃--CH₂OH\
Ethene → Ethanol

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 5. Oxidation

### Cold KMnO₄

CH₂=CH₂ → HO--CH₂--CH₂--OH\
Ethane-1,2-diol

### Hot KMnO₄

Bond cleavage → Acids / Ketones

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 6. Ozonolysis

CH₃--CH=CH₂ → CH₃CHO + HCHO\
Propene → Ethanal + Methanal

------------------------------------------------------------------------

## 7. Polymerisation

n(CH₂=CH₂) → (--CH₂--CH₂--)ₙ\
Ethene → Polythene

n(CH₃--CH=CH₂) → Polymer\
Propene → Polypropene
